C. L. Hardin

Hardin is one of the big reasons late-20th-century philosophers of color started taking color science seriously. His landmark book Color for Philosophers pushed the field to confront findings about opponent processing, variation across perceivers, and the difficulty of identifying ordinary colors like red or green with neat physical properties. Hardin is usually read as leaning toward color irrealism or eliminativism: ordinary objects are not really colored in the way they visually seem to be. A main pressure point for him is interpersonal and intrapersonal variation—there is no obviously non-arbitrary way to pick one physical reflectance type as the real red.

Nelson Goodman

Goodman is a bit different from the others because he is not mainly famous for a full-dress theory of color perception. His importance comes from two places. First, in The Structure of Appearance, he treats qualities such as colors as part of a larger project about how experience can be organized and constructed. Second, his “new riddle of induction” uses color predicates like green, blue, grue, and bleen to show that prediction is not just about evidence; it also depends on which predicates are legitimate or “projectible.” So Goodman matters for color because he shows how much our theories of color depend on classification, language, and worldmaking, not just raw sensation.

Barry Maund

Maund is both a major contributor to the philosophy of color and the author of the Stanford Encyclopedia entry on the topic. His view is often grouped with color irrealism or eliminativism. He is especially interested in the gap between the phenomenology of color—how colors appear structured in experience—and any plausible candidate in the external world. In the SEP discussion, Maund is repeatedly associated with the argument that physicalist realist theories struggle to capture the experienced unity and oppositions among colors, and he is listed among contemporary eliminativists about ordinary color properties.

Jonathan Cohen

Cohen is the leading contemporary defender of color relationalism. His core claim is that colors are not simple, monadic properties like red simpliciter or blue simpliciter. Instead, they are relational properties such as red-for-perceiver-S in circumstances-C. His main motivation is the huge amount of perceptual variation: the same object can look different across lighting conditions, perceivers, species, and contexts. Cohen’s “Master Argument” says there is no principled, non-question-begging reason to privilege one of those variants as the uniquely veridical one, so the best explanation is to relativize color to perceivers and viewing conditions.

Alex Byrne

Byrne, often working with David Hilbert, is one of the central defenders of color realism, especially a broadly physicalist version. The basic idea is that colors are objective properties of objects, typically understood in terms of reflectance-types or related physical ways of affecting light. Byrne argues that physicalism about color is much more scientifically respectable than many philosophers assumed. In the SEP summary, Byrne and Hilbert’s view is that color experiences represent objects as having certain “hue magnitudes,” and these are then explained in physical terms. So Byrne is on the realist or objectivist side against both eliminativists and strong relationalists.

Michael Tye

Tye is also a prominent objectivist realist about color, and he connects that view to his wider representationalist theory of consciousness. In broad strokes: color experience has representational content, and that content represents objective features of the world rather than merely projecting color into the mind. Like Byrne, Tye tries to preserve common-sense realism about color while still respecting color science. In the SEP discussion, Tye is grouped with objectivists and with the attempt to explain color in terms of physical properties tied to the visual system’s opponent-processing structure.
